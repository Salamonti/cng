History of Present Illness:
Mark is a 56-year-old male presenting with a long-standing history of severe, paroxysmal coughing triggered predominantly by fluid ingestion. This has been progressive over the past four to five years, initially suspected to be due to a foreign body ingestion (e.g., bristle) but no structural cause was identified on prior imaging. The coughing is now so severe that it causes near-syncopal episodes, particularly when drinking water or other fluids, and sometimes even with saliva or eating. He describes the sensation as if the fluid is triggering a reflexive cough that feels like it could cause him to lose consciousness. The cough is not associated with pain, chest discomfort, or respiratory distress, and there is no history of fever, weight loss, or other systemic symptoms. He has tried multiple interventions in the past, including medication adjustments for blood pressure, without relief. Notably, he has no history of smoking (quit 20 years ago after 10 years of smoking), no recent travel, and no occupational exposures to irritants or asbestos. Impression:
Mark presents with a chronic, progressive cough triggered by fluid ingestion, suggestive of an esophageal motility disorder such as achalasia or another form of esophageal dysmotility. The absence of esophageal dilation on CT does not rule out motility disorders, as some cases may not show classic findings. His past medical history is notable for hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, hypertension, and obstructive sleep apnea, though these are less likely to explain his symptoms. The lack of response to prior interventions, including blood pressure medication adjustments, further supports the need for a focused evaluation of esophageal function. Given the severity of his symptoms and the potential for aspiration risk, further diagnostic workup is warranted.
